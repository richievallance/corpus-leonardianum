2.5 Ratchet, Pawl, and Holding Devices

f.116r — Core gear theory: radial tooth-centre lines; defined sequence of face/flank/gap contact; strong warning: "learn the matching first or the whole attempt is vain."

f.116v — Triple-tooth pawl engagement for safety; spring-loading when vertical; danger of violent runaway and fire if pawl falls back.

f.117r — Formal definition of ritenaculo (holding wheel) and servidore (pawl/servant). First-degree good geometry: tooth line aimed at wheel centre, servant at right angle to that line, servant weighted along equidistant line. Named Florentine/Milanese vocabulary distinction.

2.6 Friction Doctrine

f.117v — True tooth flank form is to be learned from worn mill gears. Let necessity reveal the form. One of Leonardo's strongest empirical method statements.

f.118r — Short vs long pivot trade-off: short = less viscous drag; long = slower seat wear. Pyramidal wear geometry of the seat. Oiling hole self-clogging problem (filings block the hole). Multiple pivot type classification.

f.118v — Square-derived tooth construction; rounded faces; straight flanks; no universal gap rule because pairing ratios change the gap shape. "The tooth may be standardised; the gap cannot."

f.120r — Sizing and pairing: half-tooth directional correction for wheel-drives-pinion vs pinion-drives-wheel. Straight rack exception.

f.120v — Contact-sequence: point-to-point and point-to-bottom are false starts; correct approach defined by entry toward the central line.

## **f90r — CUBE TO SPHERE IMPACT DEFORMATION**

### **1. Leo's Words**

"The centre is that which is equally situated from the opposite extremes of bodies."

"Every part of the cube, pressed by the blow, flees from the centre by the shortest way."

If a very small cube were made very great by a blow, with every extremity equally dilated from the centre, it would form a circle, lacking perfection only by the original difference between the centre-to-corner distance and the centre-to-side-middle distance.

"Now see, when it had grown one braccio, what one could show as a compass-point in so great a circle."

### **2. German Cross-Check**

Confirms same centre definition and "shortest way from the centre" rule.

### **3. Geometry**

**Core solve:** Cube-to-disk shape change solved from centre outward, NOT from faces inward.

**Impact model:** Uniform blow from centre creates radial expansion. Resulting shape approaches circle but retains imperfection from cube's corner/side distance asymmetry.

### **4. Side Notes / Discrepancies**

- RWTH: sentences 3-4 are later, smaller additions
- Part of sentence 3 remains "not fully understood" per RWTH
- This is CONTINUATION of cube deformation theory from earlier folios

### **5. Backward Logic**

1. Define the centre first
2. Track all fleeing parts by shortest path
3. Only then compare corner-distance vs side-distance
4. Accept imperfection as geometric necessity

**Critical insight:** Shape change begins at centre, not surface.

## **f94v — LEVER AS SCREW ANALOGUE**

## **f100r — FRICTION: UNEQUAL TOOTH SPEED vs EQUAL BAR SPEED**

### **1. Leo's Words**

"I have found that the spindles/bars of the mill's lantern gear are often of poor durability only because they are not consumed equally. The reason is that the bars are set on perpendicular lines and their own rotation is equal in all parts of each bar, but the teeth of the driving wheel cannot move equally in all parts: the inner part of a tooth, nearer the centre, moves much less than the outer part. Therefore the faster-moving outer part presses its object more, touches it more, and so consumes it more."

Consumption happens TWO ways:
1. Top to bottom along bar
2. Around bar's circumference while contact lasts

"Sad method for durability, especially with small wheel. With large wheel or straight-line motion (rack), harm much less or none."

Wheel a consumed by two motions = not praiseworthy in small wheels. Wheel n is better.

**Ox-horn lubrication:** As it wears turns to greasy/tallow-like smoothness, doesn't abrade axle. If filled with oil, oil only flows where horn is worn by pivot, so always keeps pivot oiled.

### **2. German Cross-Check**

Confirms whole logic: unequal tooth speed, two-direction wear, horn-plus-oil lubrication.

### **3. Geometry**

**Core solve:** Wear comes from speed mismatch: different speeds across one tooth face meeting one equal-speed bar.

### **4. Side Notes / Discrepancies**

Horn turning into tallow-like self-lubricating material = striking practical note.

RWTH identifies this as beginning of broader friction/wear reduction section.

### **5. Backward Logic**

1. Before blaming material, compare speed geometry at contact
2. Change either contact form, size, or lubrication path
3. Use self-lubricating materials where possible

**Critical insight:** Friction is geometry problem, not just material problem.

## **f100v — BEARINGS (1): SPLIT ADJUSTABLE HOUSING**

### **1. Leo's Words**

"Axes that clothe one another, or rest one upon another, standing in one same socket, make various motions be made by those wheels to which they are fixed."

"Universal pivot, in all the motions of the things joined to it, and it is very strong."

"Pivot of two motions."

"Pivot of several motions."

"Pivot of two circular motions and two transversal ones, and one from above downward."

"If you wanted to make your shaft always run enclosed, and never have to leap upward, however much labour were given to it, make its cavity, or mother, of mirror-metal, that is, **3 parts copper and 7 of tin melted together**. Make this mother in two pieces, as shown in m-n, taking the shaft f in the middle. They are tightened with an iron wedge, and the rest also will be of iron. In the half of the mother above the shaft, in the lower part that touches the shaft, one may place **lead for the thickness of one finger**, and the rest all of the said metal. Little by little as it wears, tighten it again with the wedge. The same may be done with a screw in place of the wedge."

### **2. German Cross-Check**

Confirms exactly:
- Split bearing block
- Alloy recipe (3 copper : 7 tin)
- Lead contact zone
- Wedge or screw readjustment
- Fig. 1 = concentric shafts sharing one bushing

### **3. Geometry**

**Two solves at once:**
1. Multiple motions sharing one centre by nested shafts
2. Wear control by split adjustable bearing housing

**Not only freedom of motion, but controlled re-closing as bearing wears.**

### **4. Side Notes / Discrepancies**

**Material recipe is HUGE:**
- 3 copper : 7 tin (mirror-metal / speculum metal)
- Lead only where shaft touches below
- **Leonardo deliberately making bearing sacrificial in contact zone**

### **5. Backward Logic**

1. Decide the motions first
2. Decide whether shaft must never jump upward
3. Build mother in two adjustable halves
4. Let softer insert wear before shaft does

**Critical insight:** Adjustable wear control beats replacement.

## **f101v — BEARINGS (3): THREE BALLS vs FOUR, PYRAMIDAL SUSTAINERS**

### **1. Leo's Words**

"The 3 balls under their shaft are better than 4, because the 3 are certainly and necessarily always touched, and equally moved by that shaft. With 4 there would be danger that one of them might not be touched, and so it would not be moved, and it would await its companion, with which it would make rubbing."

"a will be the most praiseworthy one that can be found among these shafts, because there are **three pyramids equal among themselves**, and thus equal to the pyramid made by the point of the shaft. In such a way that in every whole turn given by the shaft, each pyramid likewise will be found to have made one whole turn. Thus they are strong and durable, **more than that which is sustained by the 3 balls, because where there is less contact there will be greater wearing, and the balls, being touched at a point, must necessarily soon be consumed**."

"These, because they have no axes, are very useful, namely f and g."

"The shaft c will make 3 turns when its sustainers make one only… each part of the sustainers is equally touched 3 times…"

"The shaft d is pitiable/bad because it is **not proportioned with its sustainers**… where the thickness of the shaft is most unlike that of the sustainers, there is contact and also the greater rubbing. And this rubbing will be almost equal to that of shaft e."

### **2. German Cross-Check**

Confirms all key points:
- 3 balls better than 4
- Conical sustainers preferred to balls
- Proportionality of shaft and sustainers is everything
- Fig. 4 fails because proportions wrong

### **3. Geometry**

**Leonardo comparing:**
- Point contact by balls
- Line/surface contact by cones/rollers
- Correct vs incorrect proportional taper

**The solve is stability + distributed contact:**
- 3 supports guarantee contact
- 4 may leave one unloaded
- Conical sustainers spread wear better than balls
- Bad taper recreates rubbing

### **4. Side Notes / Discrepancies**

"Pyramid" matters again — Leonardo reading cone/taper relation as pyramidal geometry, not merely shop trick.

Dislike of four-ball arrangement very specific: not abstract instability, but one ball losing contact and then rubbing later.

### **5. Backward Logic**

1. First choose how many support points truly stay in contact
2. Then choose whether support should touch by point or by generated cone relation
3. Then check whether taper ratios actually match

**Critical insight:** Geometry of contact beats number of points.

## **f102r — BEARINGS (4): CASCADE TO INFINITY**

### **1. Leo's Words**

"The least weight that gravity can give of itself to a suspended cord will be that which takes from it the beginning of the transversal motion. The greatest will be that of accidental motion made by perpendicular line. Therefore the shafts, loading by the perpendicular, load little in the collateral lines."

"**This invention is in the first degree of perfection, that is, of ease, because it goes to infinity.** Since every wheel that increases below acquires degrees of ease of motion, and such increase can be infinite, therefore infinitely by this means one may increase the ease of that motion."

"The turning of the wheel upon its shaft is of equal difficulty and labour to that which turns together with its shaft. I see no other advantage there except that the one which turns upon its shaft enlarges and spoils its hole and consumes the shaft. The other, which is united with the shaft, preserves its hole, and only the shaft is consumed with its own sustainer."

"Here the line of the side of shaft m-n, or if you wish m-f, because it describes a greater circle than n, would wish to move a greater quantity of its sustainer when it turns around than n does. Since such a sustainer is attached and joined together, it is impossible that such a thing have effect, because it would be necessary that the thickness of the wheel, its sustainer, be made of infinite wheels."

### **2. German Cross-Check**

Confirms big claim: Leonardo treating cascaded roller idea as tending toward **infinite ease** by repeated reduction of friction.

Also confirms wear distinction between wheel turning ON shaft vs turning TOGETHER WITH shaft.

### **3. Geometry**

**Purest anti-friction geometry folio:**
- Shift load into collateral lines
- Enlarge support stages successively
- Imagine friction being reduced without bound by cascading support members

**Last sentence important:** Outer side of thicker moving body would want to move more sustainer than inner side — becomes impossible unless sustainer itself were infinitely divisible.

### **4. Side Notes / Discrepancies**

**Folio very abstract.** Starts with weight in suspended cord, ends in near-paradox about infinitely many sustaining wheels.

NOT drift — Leonardo pushing friction reduction to conceptual limit.

### **5. Backward Logic**

1. First take load off perpendicular
2. Distribute into collateral support
3. Cascade the support
4. Test where geometry itself refuses further simplification

**Critical insight:** Friction reduction has theoretical limit where geometry paradoxes.

## **f102v — BEARINGS (5): WHOLE-NUMBER RATIOS, DESCENDING WEARS**

### **1. Leo's Words**

"That proportion which the thickness of shaft a-b has with the breadth of each wheel a-m and b-n, such will be the number of whole turns that the shaft will make when the said wheels make one whole turn. **But make the number whole, because if there were fractions it would not come out so exactly.** Although, as to movement, **the contact lines wholly change themselves in every degree of motion, and in no part of the length of such contact lines does rubbing happen**."

"The whole force of shaft f extends in transversal, that is lateral, motion, and rests upon shafts p-q."

"This is of the same nature as the one above, except that it varies in the shafts…"

"**The shaft has two contrary motions around its centre, of which one consumes itself together with its support; the other consumes neither itself nor its support**… that part which **descends toward the centre of the world consumes**; that which **moves toward the sky does not**."

"But the shaft set on a direct line consumes its lower part together with the support that is in contact with it."

### **2. German Cross-Check**

Confirms:
- Use whole-number ratios
- Contact lines change completely
- Descending loaded part wears, ascending part does not
- Vertical shaft at base wears lower contact

### **3. Geometry**

**Folio combines:**
- Conical/roller support
- Whole-number rotational relation
- Changing lines of contact
- Directional difference in wear

**Solve is NOT merely "use rollers." It is:**
- Proportion them correctly
- Let contact travel
- Know which half of rotating relation is truly burdened

### **4. Side Notes / Discrepancies**

Instruction to keep ratio whole = easy to miss, very important.

Leonardo's split between descending and ascending parts = one of sharpest wear analyses in manuscript.

### **5. Backward Logic**

1. Choose rotational ratio first
2. Make contact migrate
3. Redirect side thrust
4. Separate wearing descending side from non-wearing ascending side

**Critical insight:** Gravity direction determines wear pattern.
